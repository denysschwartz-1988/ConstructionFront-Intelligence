Fluor's JGC Fluor BC LNG II joint venture has received a limited notice to proceed for the proposed Phase 2 expansion of the LNG Canada export facility in Kitimat, British Columbia.

The milestone authorizes early planning and key activities ahead of a potential Final Investment Decision by LNG Canada's joint venture participants. The current step follows earlier FEED work and recent government co-operation measures aimed at progressing the expansion.

LNG Canada Phase 2 Background

LNG Canada is Canada's first large-scale liquefied natural gas export facility and is located in Kitimat, British Columbia, on Canada's west coast. Phase 1 has two processing trains and an annual production capacity of approximately 14 million tonnes of LNG.

The facility is owned by an LNG Canada joint venture comprising Shell at 40%, PETRONAS at 25%, PetroChina at 15%, Mitsubishi Corporation at 15% and KOGAS at 5%. Fluor said the same JGC-Fluor joint venture partners played a central role in delivering Phase 1, including engineering, procurement, fabrication management, construction and commissioning services.

According to Fluor, Phase 1 delivery included two trains and supporting infrastructure such as storage tanks, a rail yard, water treatment facility, flare stacks and marine terminal. LNG Canada Phase 2 would double the facility's production capacity if the owners reach a positive FID.

Limited Notice to Proceed Details

The limited notice to proceed has been issued to JGC Fluor BC LNG II, also referred to by Fluor as JFJV2. The Canadian joint venture is made up of Fluor Canada Ltd at 50% and JGC Constructors (No2) BC Ltd at 50%.

Fluor said the LNTP enables the joint venture to start early planning and proceed with key activities to support a proposed Phase 2 FID by LNG Canada. The company did not state a contract value for the LNTP.

Scope of Works and Delivery Status

The LNTP is not the same as full project sanction. It supports activities ahead of a possible FID, with LNG Canada's owners still required to complete their commercial, fiscal, regulatory and governance requirements before a final investment decision.

Canada's Major Projects Office page for LNG Canada Phase 2 states that the project would expand the Kitimat liquefaction facility, double production and attract an expected $33 billion in private-sector capital. Natural Resources Canada also said the LNG Canada joint venture participants approved hundreds of millions of dollars in incremental funding on May 1, 2026 to help finalize critical work scopes for a potential FID by year-end.

Coastal GasLink is also part of the wider delivery interface. TC Energy said in March 2026 that Coastal GasLink had entered commercial agreements with LNG Canada to support the pathway to FID for CGL Phase 2, including FEED and execution activities that would follow.

North American LNG Infrastructure Market Outlook

The LNG Canada LNTP adds another active pre-FID workstream to a North American LNG market already carrying large sanctioned and recently awarded projects. In Louisiana, Caturus reached FID in May 2026 for the 9.5 Mtpa Commonwealth LNG project and issued NTP to Technip Energies, while Cheniere Partners has signed a $4.69 billion EPC contract with Bechtel and issued LNTP for Sabine Pass Train 7.

Other North American LNG projects are also moving through construction or sanctioned development. Woodside's Louisiana LNG is under construction in Calcasieu Parish with a foundational phase of 16.5 Mtpa and Bechtel as EPC contractor, while Cedar LNG in Kitimat has reached FID and has early construction underway for a 3.3 Mtpa floating LNG facility expected to be in service in late 2028.

What to Watch

For contractors and suppliers, the signal is concurrency rather than a single isolated award. LNG Canada Phase 2, Sabine Pass Train 7, Commonwealth LNG, Woodside Louisiana LNG, Venture Global's CP2 LNG and Cedar LNG point to overlapping demand across liquefaction modules, marine works, cryogenic equipment, tanks, piping, insulation, instrumentation, electrical and controls work in both Western Canada and the U.S. Gulf Coast.

The strongest near-term pressure points are likely to sit with specialist contractors and supply chains that serve multiple LNG programmes at once. Fabrication capacity, long-lead equipment placement, experienced LNG construction management, commissioning teams, E&I labour, pipefitters and insulation trades are the areas to watch as projects move from LNTP, FEED and FID into heavier construction windows.

For designers, subcontractors and financiers, LNG Canada Phase 2 remains a potential late-2026 sanction event, while the related Coastal GasLink Phase 2 framework is progressing alongside terminal planning. The market question is how quickly those Canadian packages move from pre-FID scopes into committed procurement while Gulf Coast projects are already absorbing LNG delivery capacity.
